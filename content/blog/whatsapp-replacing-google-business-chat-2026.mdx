In 2026, one of the fastest-operational-impact changes to land on Google Business Profile is not a ranking tweak or a new dashboard widget. It is the quiet retirement of Business Profile chat. Google is winding down the native messaging and call-history features that lived inside the Business Profile and redirecting customer conversations toward established channels — with WhatsApp as the obvious destination in markets like India, where it is already the default way customers talk to businesses.

For most Indian SMBs this is not a loss. Google's in-profile chat was always a lightly used, easy-to-miss inbox. The risk is not that you lose a great channel; it is that you lose an inbound path you never fully replaced, and the customer who would have messaged you simply messages a competitor instead. This guide covers exactly what is changing, why Google is making the move, and the migration steps that turn the disruption into an advantage — specifically, into a review-collection and recovery engine.

## What is changing, and the timeline

The change is narrow but important. Your Business Profile — the listing that shows up in Google Search and Maps with your hours, photos, reviews, and directions — stays exactly as it is. What is going away is the **messaging surface inside it**: the chat button that let a customer message you from within Google, and the associated call-history view.

Practically, that means:

- The "Chat" / "Message" action that some profiles showed is being removed.
- Conversations that started inside Google will no longer have a home there.
- Google is nudging businesses to surface their own messaging channels instead — website chat, phone, and increasingly a direct WhatsApp link.

If you never used Business Profile chat, the only thing you need to do is make sure customers have an obvious, fast way to reach you that you actually monitor. If you did use it, you have a real migration to run before the cutover finishes, because every day without a replacement is a day of missed inbound.

## Why Google is moving messaging to WhatsApp

Three forces explain the shift, and understanding them tells you where to invest.

**1. Google was a weak messaging operator.** Running a real-time consumer messaging product is expensive and demands relentless spam control, notification reliability, and mobile presence. Google has repeatedly started and stopped messaging products. Business Profile chat never reached the scale or reliability of the dedicated messengers, and maintaining a half-used inbox is hard to justify.

**2. Customers already left.** In India especially, the conversation already happens on WhatsApp. Customers DM a salon, a clinic, a coaching centre, or a kirana-adjacent retailer on WhatsApp before they ever consider a website form or an in-Google chat. Google is following the user, not leading them.

**3. The listing is the asset; the inbox is a feature.** Google's strategic value is the discovery layer — being the place customers find you. Owning the ongoing one-to-one conversation is lower value and higher cost. Handing messaging to channels customers prefer lets Google focus on discovery while keeping the profile sticky.

The takeaway: do not try to recreate the in-Google inbox somewhere else. Move to where the customer already is.

## What SMBs must do now to migrate

Run this as a short checklist, not a project. Most businesses can finish it in an afternoon.

1. **Publish a WhatsApp click-to-chat link.** Create a `wa.me` link with your business number and a pre-filled greeting. Put it on your website, your Business Profile's website/appointment fields where allowed, your Instagram bio, and your invoices and receipts.
2. **Designate who answers, and how fast.** A channel nobody watches is worse than no channel. Assign an owner and a target first-response time — under an hour during business hours is the bar Indian customers now expect.
3. **Set up WhatsApp Business properly.** For low volume, the free WhatsApp Business app with quick replies and away messages is enough. Past roughly 30–50 conversations a day, move to the WhatsApp Cloud API so you can route, automate, and integrate.
4. **Capture the number at every interaction.** The phone number is the asset that makes the next two stages possible. Collect it at checkout, booking, and service completion.
5. **Audit your old Google chat threads.** If you used Business Profile chat, export or note any open conversations and proactively move those customers to WhatsApp before the surface disappears.

## Turn WhatsApp into a review-collection and recovery channel

Here is where the migration stops being defensive and starts paying for itself. The same channel that replaces Google chat is, in India, the single best channel for collecting Google reviews.

The mechanics are simple and the timing is everything. After a positive interaction — a completed service, a delivered order, a finished appointment — send a short WhatsApp message within a few hours:

> Hi {First Name}, thanks for visiting {Business} today. If you had a good experience, a quick Google review would mean a lot: {short link}. — {Owner}

Why this beats every other channel in India:

- **It is read.** WhatsApp open rates dwarf email, where local-business asks convert at low single digits.
- **It is one tap.** A direct link to the Google review form removes the friction that kills conversion.
- **It carries context.** The customer just talked to you on the same thread, so the ask feels personal, not robotic.

WhatsApp is also a **recovery** channel, not just a collection channel. When a customer is unhappy, a private WhatsApp conversation lets you resolve the problem before it becomes a public one-star review — and lets you invite a rating update after you have fixed the issue. Routing dissatisfaction into a private fix-it thread, while still letting genuinely happy customers post publicly, is the heart of a healthy review operation. ## The India playbook

India is the market where this transition matters most, because WhatsApp's SMB reach here is close to universal. A few India-specific moves separate the businesses that gain from this change from those that merely survive it.

**Lead with the customer's language.** A salon in Pune, a coaching centre in Indore, a clinic in Coimbatore — each serves customers who think in different languages. Your WhatsApp greeting, your review ask, and your recovery messages should match the language the customer used. A Hindi review ask to a Hindi-speaking customer and a Tamil ask to a Tamil-speaking one convert measurably better than an English-only template.

**Use templates, but never blast.** WhatsApp's strength is that it feels personal. Pre-approved templates keep you fast, but sending bulk messages to people with no recent interaction is the quickest way to tank your quality rating and your reputation. Message customers you actually served, close to when you served them.

**Tie WhatsApp to the review loop, not just support.** Many Indian SMBs already use WhatsApp for orders and queries but never connect it to reviews. The businesses winning local search in 2026 treat every resolved WhatsApp conversation as a review opportunity — happy customers to Google, unhappy ones into a private recovery thread first.

**Respect the 24-hour window.** Inside 24 hours of a customer message, replies are free-form and free. Outside it, you can only send pre-approved templates, which are billed. Structuring your collection and recovery flows around that window keeps costs near zero while staying compliant.

## The bottom line

Google retiring Business Profile chat is not a setback — it is Google formalising what already happened. The conversation moved to WhatsApp years ago. The businesses that treat this as a prompt to build a real WhatsApp operation — fast replies, language-matched asks, review collection on the happy path, private recovery on the unhappy one — will come out of 2026 with a stronger inbound channel and more Google reviews than they started with. The ones who wait for the cutover to force their hand will spend a quarter losing inbound to faster competitors.
