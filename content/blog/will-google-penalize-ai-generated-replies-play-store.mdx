## The direct answer

No. Google's Developer Comment Posting Policy prohibits the following categories of reply content:

- Spam, including repeated or templated comments.
- Manipulation, including coordinated rating or review schemes.
- Off-topic content.
- Illegal content.
- Hate speech, harassment, or threats.
- Impersonation of users or other parties.
- Promotional content unrelated to the user's review.

AI authorship is **not on that list.** The policy applies to the content and pattern of the reply, not who or what generated it. A reply written by a junior support rep using a fill-in-the-blank template can be flagged. A reply written by GPT-4 with proper context cannot — if it is varied, contextual, and stays in policy.

The policy text is publicly available in the Google Play Developer Program Policy Center. Read it directly. Every claim in this post comes from that source.

## What Google ACTUALLY penalizes

Five concrete patterns trigger reply-spam detection:

### 1. Templated replies that don't address the review

The biggest single flag. A reply that says "Thank you for your feedback, we will look into this" on a 1-star review complaining about a specific bug is exactly the pattern Google's quality systems are trained to detect. The Play algorithm and the spam classifier both flag low-quality engagement.

ChatGPT defaults to this kind of generic output when given a thin prompt. With proper prompt engineering (covered in [Best ChatGPT prompts for Play Store review replies](/blog/best-chatgpt-prompts-for-play-store-review-replies)) the output improves, but the failure mode is templated genericness.

### 2. Identical replies posted in bulk

Posting the exact same reply text on 50 reviews in an hour is the clearest spam pattern. Google's automated systems catch this fast. Even posting near-identical replies (changing the user's name but nothing else) gets flagged.

This is where AI tools actually help: a properly configured tool generates variations automatically. Posting different replies on every review is harder by hand than with AI assistance.

### 3. Replies that violate user privacy

Mentioning personal details from the review publicly — health conditions, address fragments, account numbers — can get the reply removed regardless of intent. The Developer Comment Posting Policy explicitly covers this.

The rule: never repeat personal information in a public reply. If a user mentions their email in the review, do not address them by it; route them to a private channel.

### 4. Replies containing prohibited content

A few categories:

- URLs to non-support resources. Linking to your marketing site, a blog post, or a partner is flagged. Linking to your support email or help center is fine.
- Promotional content. "Try our new feature, only $9.99" in a reply is a policy violation.
- Profanity or hate speech. Obvious but bears stating.
- Defensive responses naming third parties. "Our competitor [Name] has the same bug" is libelous risk territory.

### 5. Bulk activity that looks bot-driven

Replying to 100 reviews in a 60-second window is bot-like behavior, regardless of whether the replies vary. Google's behavioral systems flag this even if the content is fine. The fix is to throttle — well-tuned AI reply tools add a 30-90 second jitter between replies to look human, never burst-post.

## The April 2026 Google Play Policy update

There was an April 2026 update to Google Play's policy framework that introduced new disclosure rules for AI-generated content. The actual scope:

- Apps using AI to generate or modify media (deepfakes, AI-synthesized voices, AI-modified photos) now must disclose this in their listing.
- Apps generating user-facing AI content must include user-reporting mechanisms.
- Apps must label AI-generated outputs visibly to end users.

The scope does **not** include developer replies on the Play Store. The policy is about content shown to end users inside apps, not about how developers communicate on the store-facing review section. This is widely misquoted in indie developer forums — you will see posts claiming "Google now requires you to disclose AI replies." That is not what the policy says.

Read the actual policy text from Google Play Console > Policy Center > AI-Generated Content Policy. The wording is specific to in-app AI content, not store-listing developer replies.

## The 4 rules for AI replies that stay within policy

If you want a simple checklist to give your team:

### Rule 1 — Always reference the specific review

Generic "Thanks for your feedback" is the #1 quality flag. Every reply should mention something specific from the original review — the feature complained about, the bug experienced, the version number, the use case. If your AI tool is not pulling this context, change tools.

### Rule 2 — Vary your replies

Never paste the same AI output across multiple reviews. If your tool generates one suggested reply per review, generate three and pick the best, or rotate variations across the day. This is the single biggest defense against the bulk-identical-reply flag.

### Rule 3 — Stay under 350 characters

The Play Store hard-limits replies to 350 characters. Truncation looks unprofessional and a long-then-cut reply looks careless. Use the [Play Store Character Counter](/tools/play-store-character-counter) for one-off checks or pick a tool that enforces the limit automatically.

### Rule 4 — Avoid prohibited content

No URLs except support email or help center. No marketing language. No defensive responses that name competitors. No incentives ("comment again for a free month"). If your AI tool ever drafts any of these, it is the wrong tool.

## What COULD get you in trouble

Three things will get you actioned, regardless of whether AI was involved:

- **Using AI to write fake user reviews.** A completely different policy violation under the Fake Engagement section. AI assisting your real replies is fine; AI generating fake reviews on your own app is a near-certain account suspension.
- **AI auto-replying within seconds of a review posting.** Looks bot-driven. Even if Google's policy did not explicitly flag this, your users notice and your replies look hollow. Add a deliberate 5-30 minute delay before AI replies fire.
- **Incentivizing users in replies.** "Comment again for a discount" is a Developer Program Policy violation regardless of who wrote it. The AI tool you use should not even let you do this.
