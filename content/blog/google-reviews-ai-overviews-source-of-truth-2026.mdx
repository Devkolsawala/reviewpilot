The biggest shift in local discovery in 2026 is not where customers search but how the answer is assembled. When someone searches for a dentist in Bandra, a CA in Koramangala, or the best biryani in Hyderabad, Google increasingly answers with an AI Overview — a Gemini-generated summary that recommends specific businesses before the map pack even loads. And the evidence that summary is built from is your customer reviews.

This matters because it changes the unit of competition. For two decades, local SEO was about signals you fed Google: categories, citations, keywords, links. AI Overviews add a new layer on top — a model that reads your reviews as a body of evidence and decides, in natural language, whether to recommend you and what to say. Reviews stopped being social proof you hope a human reads. They became the **source of truth** an AI reasons over. This post is about how to make that source of truth work in your favour.

A note on scope: optimising apps and SaaS products to be surfaced by generative engines is a related but separate craft, covered in [generative engine optimization for apps and SaaS](/blog/generative-engine-optimization-for-apps-and-saas-2026). This article is specifically about local and Maps discovery — where customer reviews are the grounding layer.

## What AI Overviews changed in discovery

Before AI Overviews, a local search returned a ranked list and the customer did the synthesis — scanning ratings, reading a few reviews, forming a judgement. Now the model does the synthesis first and presents a conclusion: "Three well-reviewed options stand out..." with a sentence on each.

Two things follow from that.

**First, the model needs evidence to make a claim.** It will not assert that your clinic "is praised for short wait times and a gentle paediatric team" unless reviews say something close to that. The recommendation is grounded in text it can point to. No supporting reviews, no claim, no citation.

**Second, the synthesis compresses the funnel.** Customers used to read several listings; now many read one AI paragraph and act. Being named in that paragraph is worth more than ranking fourth in a list nobody scrolls. And being named is decided largely by the quality and specificity of your review corpus.

## Why reviews are the grounding layer

Generative models are prone to making things up. To keep AI Overviews trustworthy, Google grounds them in retrievable, attributable content — and for a local business, the richest such content is its reviews. Reviews are recent, specific, written by real customers, and tied to your verified profile. That makes them ideal grounding material: the model can lean on them with confidence and, in effect, cite them.

This is why two businesses with the same 4.5-star average can be treated completely differently by an AI Overview. One has 200 reviews that say "great," "nice," "good service." The other has 200 reviews that say "Dr. Mehta explained my root canal step by step," "they fit me in same-day for a cracked filling," "billing was transparent and itemised." The second business has given the model specific, themeable, citable evidence. The first has given it noise. The model recommends the one it can describe.

Rating still matters as a floor, but above that floor, **specificity beats average**. The detail in your reviews is what the AI has to work with.

## Making your reviews AI-readable

You cannot write your customers' reviews, but you can shape the conditions that produce specific, themeable, well-answered ones. Three levers do most of the work.

### 1. Engineer specificity at the ask

Generic asks produce generic reviews. When you request a review, prompt for detail without scripting it: "If you have a minute, mention what you came in for and how it went." A customer nudged to name the service, the staff member, or the outcome writes exactly the kind of concrete sentence an AI Overview can quote. You are not gaming the model; you are helping satisfied customers say something useful.

### 2. Manage themes, not just stars

AI Overviews summarise by theme — wait time, expertise, value, cleanliness, outcome. If you do not know which themes dominate your reviews, you cannot tell whether the model has good material to ground a recommendation. Reading your review corpus by theme is the single most useful diagnostic here; [how to read app review sentiment analysis](/blog/how-to-read-app-review-sentiment-analysis) lays out the method for surfacing and tracking themes, and the same approach applies directly to Google reviews. If a theme you want to be known for is thin, that is where to focus collection.

### 3. Answer every review — owner replies are grounding too

Owner replies are read by the model as part of the thread. A reply that confirms a fix ("we have added a second receptionist for weekend mornings"), names a service, or clarifies a misunderstanding adds context the AI can use. An answered thread reads as more complete and more trustworthy than a bare review. Beyond the AI, replying fast is already a documented local ranking and conversion lever — the full data on how reply rate and latency move Maps rank is in [AI review replies and Google Maps ranking](/blog/ai-review-replies-google-maps-ranking-2026).

## A reply strategy built for AI grounding

If reviews and replies are the grounding layer, your reply discipline is no longer just customer service — it is content production for the AI. A reply strategy tuned for this does three things.

**Add the fact the review left out.** If a five-star review says only "great service," your reply can supply the specifics the customer did not: "So glad the express GST filing worked out, Anil — thanks for trusting us with the quarterly return." Now the thread contains the theme even if the review did not.

**Resolve and document complaints publicly.** When you fix a problem, say so in the reply. The model — and the next human reader — sees a business that responds and improves. An unresolved complaint with no reply is the worst possible grounding material; a resolved one with a clear reply is reassurance.

**Keep it specific and human.** Templated "Thank you for your feedback!" replies add nothing the model can use and are visibly robotic to humans. Vary them, reference the actual review, and keep them short.

Doing this across dozens of reviews a week by hand is where it breaks down for most teams. AI-assisted replies — drafted in your voice, referencing the specific review, then approved by a human — let you maintain this discipline at volume without it becoming a part-time job.

## The India angle

For Indian SMBs, two factors raise the stakes and the opportunity.

**Language and code-mixing.** A large share of Indian reviews are in Hindi, Tamil, Telugu, Marathi, or code-mixed Hinglish. AI Overviews can read and summarise these, but only if your review corpus actually contains them. Encouraging and replying to reviews in the customer's language enriches the grounding material in exactly the languages your prospective customers search in. Replying in the same language compounds the effect.

**Category whitespace.** In many Indian local categories, competitors still treat reviews as a vanity number — collecting stars, ignoring text, never replying. That leaves the AI with thin grounding for the whole category. A single business that builds a specific, themed, well-answered review corpus can become the default recommendation simply because it is the only one giving the model something concrete to say.

## The bottom line

AI Overviews turned reviews from social proof into source material. The businesses that win local discovery in 2026 are not necessarily the ones with the highest average — they are the ones whose reviews give Gemini specific, recent, themed, well-answered evidence to ground a recommendation in. That is a content problem disguised as a reviews problem, and it is solvable: ask for specificity, manage your themes, and answer every review like the next reader is a machine deciding whether to recommend you.
