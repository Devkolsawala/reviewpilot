## 1. The Android In-App Review API: what it is and how it works

The In-App Review API is a Google Play Core library (`com.google.android.play:review`) that lets you show a native review prompt inside your app — users rate and write a review without leaving. Conversion is dramatically higher than asking users to navigate to the Play Store manually.

The basic flow is three calls:

```kotlin
val manager = ReviewManagerFactory.create(context)
val request = manager.requestReviewFlow()

request.addOnCompleteListener { task ->
  if (task.isSuccessful) {
    val flow = manager.launchReviewFlow(activity, task.result)
    flow.addOnCompleteListener { /* prompt closed */ }
  }
}
```

Two things to know up front. First, Google enforces an opaque quota — you can request the prompt many times, but Google only actually shows it to users a few times per year. Your callback success doesn't tell you whether the dialog appeared, only that the request was processed. Second, the prompt looks identical to Google's native one; you can't customize its appearance, and that's by design — users trust it because it's not a third-party interruption.

Plan around this: don't try to "spam" the API. Trigger it once at a carefully chosen high-emotion moment.

## 2. The "happy moment" rule

The single biggest determinant of whether a user leaves a 5-star review is when you ask. Asking after a frustrating moment gets you 1 stars. Asking after a frustrating moment that the user just resolved gets you 5 stars. The emotional arc matters more than any clever copy.

Real-world examples of well-timed prompts:

- **Notion** — triggers the review prompt after a user creates their 7th note. The user has crossed the activation threshold and feels competent. Not on launch, not on signup.
- **Duolingo** — prompts after a 14-day streak. Streaks are the app's emotional peak; users feel pride and want to validate it.
- **Headspace** — asks after the third completed meditation session. The user has experienced the product's value three times.
- **Zerodha Kite** — prompts after the user's first successful trade settlement, which is the moment of "this actually works."

The common pattern: ask after a small win that proves your value. Never ask at app launch — the user has no signal yet and the prompt feels like a tax.

## 3. Why asking too early kills you

Asking on app launch, after onboarding, or before the user has experienced value is the most common mistake we see. Three reasons it backfires:

First, you waste your quota. Google's In-App Review API only fires the prompt a few times per user per year, total. Burn it on a cold launch and you can't fire it again at a happy moment.

Second, you train users to dismiss the prompt. A user who sees a review request before they care about your app dismisses it, and Google's prompt-suppression logic remembers that — the next prompt is even less likely to appear.

Third, you get bad ratings. A user with no investment in your product who's annoyed by a prompt rates you 1-star out of spite. This is a real pattern; we've seen apps drop 0.4 stars in a month after enabling launch-time prompts.

The fix is brutally simple: instrument your app, find the moment users say "wow" in usage data, and prompt only then.

## 4. NPS pre-screening: route promoters, deflect detractors

The smartest review-acquisition pattern in 2026 is NPS pre-screening. The flow:

1. After a high-emotion moment, show a one-question survey: "How likely are you to recommend this app to a friend? 1–10."
2. If the user picks 9 or 10 (a "promoter"), immediately fire the In-App Review API.
3. If the user picks 0–8 (a "detractor" or "passive"), route them to a private feedback form. Ask what would have made it a 10. Reply within 24 hours.

This is fully compliant with Google's review policy because you're not blocking anyone — every user can still leave a public review by going to Play Store directly. You're just routing your in-app prompt to the people most likely to leave a 5-star.

Apps that adopt this pattern consistently see their rating climb 0.3–0.6 stars in 90 days. The math is obvious: you stop forcing detractors to leave a public review, and you fast-track promoters who would otherwise forget.

Pair this with [a fast Play Store reply process](/blog/how-to-respond-to-play-store-reviews-fast) for the detractors who do leave a public review anyway. Speed of reply is what flips 1-stars to 4-stars.

## 5. The 24-hour reply rule

Reply velocity is a measurable ranking signal — apps with high reply rates on negative reviews outrank apps with similar ratings but slower replies. The mechanism appears to be twofold: users seeing a fast, thoughtful response sometimes update their rating, and Google's algorithm rewards engagement signals.

The bar is concrete:

- Reply to every 1- and 2-star review within 4 hours during business hours, 24 hours otherwise.
- Reply to every 3-star within 48 hours.
- Reply to at least 50% of 4- and 5-star reviews. They convert into advocates.
- Never use identical copy-paste replies. Google's spam detection flags them and users call them out in the comments.

Most app teams underestimate the volume this requires. If your app gets 200 reviews a week, that's 80+ replies a week, all under 350 characters, all unique. ## 6. The festival and seasonal cadence (India-specific)

Indian users leave more reviews during festive periods. The pattern is consistent across categories:

- **Diwali (October–November)** — review volume spikes 40–60% in the two weeks around the festival. Users are spending more on apps and feel generous. A festive in-app prompt with a small offer ("rate us and unlock our Diwali sale") works disproportionately well — note that the offer must be tied to the act of rating, not the rating itself, to stay policy-compliant.
- **End of financial year (March)** — fintech and productivity apps see a spike as users review apps they used heavily during tax season.
- **New Year (January)** — health, fitness, and habit apps benefit from resolution-driven engagement.
- **Monsoon and exam season** — education and weather apps spike.

Plan a quarterly prompt-timing review. Apps that align review asks to seasonal peaks add 20–30% more reviews per quarter than those that ignore timing.

## 7. What KILLS your Play Store rating

Most rating damage doesn't come from a bad product — it comes from operational mistakes that betray user trust. The seven most damaging:

**Silent feature removal.** You drop a feature in v4.0 because 0.5% of users used it. That 0.5% leaves 1-stars and tells their networks. Always announce removals two weeks ahead, in-app.

**Surprise paywalls.** Adding a paywall to a previously-free feature triggers a 1-star wave within 48 hours. If you have to monetize, grandfather existing users for at least 30 days.

**Permission creep.** Asking for new permissions (contacts, microphone, location) in an update spooks users. Explain why, link to your privacy doc, and make it optional where possible.

**Aggressive in-app ads.** Free-tier users tolerate ads up to a point. Going from one ad per session to one every two screens loses you a star fast.

**Push notification spam.** Sending more than 1–2 daily notifications drives uninstalls and 1-star reviews from users who feel manipulated. Personalize and reduce.

**Breaking changes without warning.** A sync change, an account migration, a UI overhaul — anything that requires user action is a 1-star magnet if it's not telegraphed in advance.

**Slow or absent support.** A 1-star review citing "no response from support" is the kind of review that compounds — other angry users pile on the same comment thread.

The mental model: rating recovery is slow, rating damage is fast. Treat every product decision through the lens of "will this surprise a user and prompt a 1-star?"
