A pattern is showing up across Indian local categories in 2026 that confuses a lot of business owners: a long-established business — strong brand, years of reviews, a profile that ranked in the map pack for as long as anyone can remember — quietly slides down the results, and a newer competitor takes its spot. Nothing changed about the established business. That is exactly the problem. Nothing changed, and in 2026, nothing changing is the fastest way to lose local rank.

The cause is a shift in how Google's local algorithm weights its signals. The old model rewarded **prominence** — accumulated authority, the sense that a business is well-known and well-established. The 2026 model leans much harder on **popularity** — live evidence that customers are engaging with the business right now. Prominence is a stock; popularity is a flow. And when the algorithm starts pricing the flow over the stock, a profile that stops moving starts to decay.

## The prominence-to-popularity shift, explained

Prominence was always part of Google's local ranking, alongside relevance and distance. It captured how well-known a business is — review count and rating, links, citations, articles, offline reputation. It rewarded history, which is why an established business with a decade of reviews could coast near the top for years.

The problem with prominence as a dominant signal is that it goes stale. A business can be prominent on paper and dead in practice — closed on the days it lists as open, slow to answer, no fresh reviews in a year. Recommending it produces a bad customer experience, which is the one thing Google's local product cannot afford.

So the weighting moved toward popularity: signals that prove the business is **alive and chosen right now**. Are customers clicking to call? Requesting directions? Leaving and reading reviews this month? Is the owner replying? Are there recent photos and posts? Popularity is harder to fake and far more predictive of whether a customer who picks you will have a good experience. The model trusts it more — and so historical authority no longer shields a profile that has gone quiet.

## The ~30-day decay

There is no official number, but the field-observed pattern is a roughly 30-day window. A profile that generates no fresh engagement for about a month — no new reviews, no owner replies, no posts, flat customer actions — begins to see its impressions soften. The decline is not a cliff; it is a slow bleed that compounds. Month two is worse than month one. By the time the owner notices the drop in calls and walk-ins, the profile has often been quietly decaying for a quarter.

The cruel part is that the businesses most exposed are the established ones. They built their position in the prominence era, when ranking was something you earned once and kept. They never built an activity habit because they never needed one. Meanwhile a newer competitor, with no historical authority to lean on, is forced to generate engagement constantly — and that constant activity is now exactly what the algorithm rewards. The result is the inversion owners keep reporting: the new business outranking the old one.

## What actually counts as "engagement"

If popularity is the lever, you need to know what feeds it. Engagement is not one number; it is a basket of live signals:

- **Customer actions on the listing** — clicks to call, website clicks, direction requests, and the dwell and click-through behaviour around your result.
- **Review activity** — new reviews arriving, and just as importantly, owner replies going out. A two-sided, recent review thread is a strong liveness signal.
- **Posts and offers** — Business Profile posts, updates, and offers that show the listing is being maintained.
- **Photos** — fresh images from the business and from customers.
- **Q&A activity** — questions asked and answered.
- **Information freshness** — accurate hours, seasonal updates, and prompt edits when something changes.

Notice how many of these are within your control and cost nothing but discipline. You cannot manufacture customer clicks directly, but you can drive the review and reply activity that signals liveness — and that, in turn, lifts the conversion and click behaviour that drives the rest.

## The review-reply engagement angle

Of all the engagement signals, the review-and-reply loop is the one with the best leverage for most SMBs, for three reasons.

**It is recurring by nature.** Reviews arrive continuously if you ask for them, and every reply you post is a fresh, dated engagement event. A business that collects a few reviews a week and replies to each within a day generates a steady drumbeat of exactly the signal the popularity model wants.

**It compounds into the other signals.** Fresh, well-answered reviews lift conversion — more of the people who see your listing choose it — which produces more clicks, calls, and direction requests, which are themselves top-tier engagement signals. The review loop seeds the whole basket.

**It is the hardest for a quiet competitor to match.** An established business that has gone silent can switch posts back on in an afternoon, but rebuilding a live review stream takes weeks of disciplined asking and replying. That lead time is your moat if you start now and theirs if they do.

## A 2026 Google Business Profile activity checklist

Treat the profile like a living surface, not a set-and-forget listing. The minimum cadence that keeps a profile out of the decay zone:

- **Weekly:** ask recent happy customers for a review; reply to every new review within 24–48 hours; post one update, offer, or photo.
- **Monthly:** review your customer-action trend in the profile dashboard; refresh photos; answer any open Q&A; confirm hours and info are current.
- **Quarterly:** audit which engagement signals are flat and pick the weakest to push; check whether a competitor has overtaken you and why.
- **Always:** keep hours accurate, especially around Indian festivals and seasonal closures — a profile marked open when you are shut produces bad customer experiences the algorithm punishes.

None of this is heavy. The whole point is that small, consistent activity beats large, occasional bursts. A profile that does a little every week stays alive in the model's eyes; a profile that does a big push once a year and then goes silent decays between pushes.

## The India angle

Indian SMBs are unusually exposed to this shift for a simple reason: many built strong profiles early, when local competition online was thin, and then stopped maintaining them as the business ran on word of mouth and repeat customers. That worked while prominence ruled. It fails under popularity, because a newer, hungrier competitor — a second clinic, a new salon, a fresh coaching centre — is generating the daily engagement the algorithm now rewards.

The fix is also easier in India than almost anywhere, because the collection channel is already in your customers' hands. WhatsApp gives you a near-universal way to ask recent customers for reviews within hours of service, in their own language. A business that turns even a fraction of its daily footfall into a steady review-and-reply stream generates more liveness signal than most local competitors combined.

## The bottom line

The rules of local search changed from "earn authority once" to "prove you are alive every month." An established profile is no longer safe by virtue of its history, and a newer one is no longer locked out by lack of it. Impressions now follow engagement, and engagement decays in roughly 30 days of silence. The businesses that win in 2026 are the ones that build a small weekly activity habit — most of it review collection and replies — and never let the profile go quiet.
