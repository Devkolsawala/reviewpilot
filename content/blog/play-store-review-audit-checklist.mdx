Most founders never actually audit their Play Store reviews. They scroll the latest reviews tab in Play Console, react to whichever one is loudest, and tell themselves they are on top of things. They are not.

A real review audit is a five-minute structured diagnostic that answers four questions: how bad is the silence, where is the pain concentrated, what is the next thing to fix, and is the rating about to move up or down. You can do it weekly. You should do it weekly. Here is the checklist.

## The 5-minute audit checklist

Run this in order. Do not skip steps even when you think you already know the answer.

1. **Open the [Play Store review analyzer](/tools/play-store-analyzer) and paste your app's Play Store URL.** Wait for the output.
2. **Note your displayed rating and total review count.** Compare to the same numbers from your last audit. Note the delta.
3. **Note your response rate.** Both overall and on negative reviews (1 to 3 star) specifically.
4. **Count unreplied negative reviews from the last 30 days.** This is the most actionable number in the audit.
5. **Read your top 3 complaint themes.** Tag each one as product, pricing, support, or trust.
6. **Read your top 3 praise themes.** Confirm they still match your marketing positioning.
7. **Scan the last 20 reviews verbatim.** Look for new patterns the theme clustering may have missed.
8. **Write three decisions.** One thing to reply to today, one thing to investigate this week, one thing to ship this sprint.

That is the audit. The numbers are means, not ends. Steps 1 through 7 only matter because they let you write step 8 with confidence. Most teams skip straight to step 8 and ship the wrong thing.

## How to interpret your response rate

Response rate is a leading indicator of both rating direction and ranking position. The [reply rate ranking signal](/blog/review-reply-rate-play-store-ranking-signal) is now well-documented inside Google Play's category algorithms, but the audit number you care about is simpler.

Three thresholds to memorize:

**Negative reply rate below 50 percent.** You are in active damage. Every angry user you ignored is publishing their grievance for every future installer to read. The damage is not the past review — it is every prospective install who reads that thread next month. Fix the reply backlog before you ship anything else.

**Negative reply rate 50 to 79 percent.** You are inconsistent. Replies are happening when someone has time. The audit signal is "this is not yet a system." Set a daily 15-minute slot, draft from a template library, and the number moves to 85 percent within two weeks without any new tooling.

**Negative reply rate 80 percent or higher.** You are operating well. Now focus on reply *quality* — not generic templates, real acknowledgement of the specific complaint, real follow-up on bugs. The marginal lift moves from "did you reply" to "did your reply actually change the reviewer's behavior."

Overall reply rate matters too, but less. A 4 or 5 star review usually does not need a multi-paragraph response. A one-sentence thank-you is enough and is mostly there for the next reader, not the reviewer.

## How to interpret your unreplied negative count

If only one number from this audit makes it into a weekly standup, it is this one. Unreplied negatives in the last 30 days.

Here is the calibration:

- **0 unreplied.** Excellent. Move to product work.
- **1 to 5 unreplied.** Acceptable noise. Clear before end of week.
- **6 to 15 unreplied.** Backlog forming. Block a half-day this week.
- **16 to 40 unreplied.** Active problem. This is the next thing the team works on.
- **More than 40 unreplied.** Red flag. The compounding install loss is now visible in your conversion data even if you have not connected the dots.

Worth knowing: each unreplied 1-star review costs roughly 2 to 4 future installs over its visible lifetime. The math is in the [unanswered reviews revenue loss calculation](/blog/unanswered-reviews-revenue-loss-calculation). For an app at 10,000 installs a month, 40 unreplied 1-stars is approximately 80 to 160 lost installs every month they sit there. At a typical ₹40 to ₹120 install acquisition cost in India, that is real money walking out of the building because someone did not block 30 minutes a week.

## How to interpret your top complaint themes

The analyzer will surface 5 to 8 complaint clusters. The audit job is to tag each one and decide whether it is durable or transient.

Tag each theme on three dimensions:

**Category.** Product, pricing, support, trust, performance, or localization. A theme tagged "trust" — for example "feels like a scam," "took my money," "no one responds" — is the most expensive to ignore because it kills installs from people who never even tried your app.

**Recency.** Has the theme shown up consistently for 90+ days, or only in the last 30? Persistent themes need engineering or policy fixes. Recent-only themes might be a regression from your last release.

**Severity.** Single-line complaints versus 500-word essays. Long complaints almost always come from users who *wanted* the product to work. They are higher recovery value than the dismissive one-liners.

A useful audit shorthand looks like this:

> Theme 1: "OTP fails on Jio numbers." [product / persistent / severe] — engineer this week.
> Theme 2: "Subscription auto-renewed without warning." [trust / recent / severe] — policy fix + reply backlog clear by Friday.
> Theme 3: "App slow after Android 15 update." [performance / recent / medium] — investigate, may be fixed by upcoming SDK update.

Three tagged themes is enough to drive a week's prioritization. Do not let the audit become a six-page document — it should fit on half a page.

## When the audit tells you to fix product vs reply harder vs both

The most common audit outcome for an Indian app rated between 3.6 and 4.1 is a mix. The instinct is to either rage-fix product or rage-reply to everyone. Neither alone works. Here is the decision rule.

**Fix product first when:** the top 3 complaint themes are all product-tagged, persistent, and concentrated in the last 60 days. Reply quality cannot save you from a broken core flow. Ship the fix, then catch up replies during QA.

**Reply harder first when:** the top complaint themes are trust-tagged and the actual product complaints are scattered. You are bleeding from feeling-ignored more than from feeling-broken. Two weeks of disciplined 24-hour replies, paired with public roadmap updates in your replies, moves the displayed rating measurably without shipping a single feature.

**Do both in parallel when:** you have one persistent, severe product complaint *and* one trust complaint cluster. Most apps recovering from a 1-star burst after a bad release are in this state. The product fix takes a sprint. The trust repair can start today.

If you cannot tell which bucket you are in, the [how to recover app rating 2 stars to 4 stars](/blog/how-to-recover-app-rating-2-stars-to-4-stars) playbook walks through the diagnostic in more detail. But for a weekly audit, the three-bucket rule above is enough.

## Running the audit weekly without it taking over your week

The audit is supposed to take five minutes. If it is taking 90 minutes, you are not auditing — you are spiraling. Two operational rules to keep the cadence sustainable.

**Audit on a fixed day, fixed time.** Friday 4 PM is a popular slot because the week's release cycle has settled and weekend reviews have not yet flooded in. Whatever you pick, do not move it. Skipped audits cost more than a missed standup because the data only stays meaningfully recent for about seven days.

**Decisions, not feelings.** Every audit ends with three written decisions. If an audit produced "we should think about pricing," that is not a decision — that is an unresolved thought. Either it becomes "test ₹49 monthly tier next sprint" or it becomes "no action this week." Open loops kill weekly audits faster than anything.

The third rule is informal but matters: **share the audit with at least one other person.** A co-founder, a senior engineer, an investor advisor. Five minutes of "here is what I am seeing" forces clearer thinking than reading the analyzer output alone. Most teams that maintain a 12-month weekly-audit habit have someone they ping after every run.

A side benefit of the cadence: when something dramatic does happen — a 1-star burst from a bad rollout, a competitor PR moment, a viral negative thread — you have eight weeks of audit history to compare against. Your reaction is calibrated, not panicked. That alone justifies the time.

## Run your first audit today

If you have not audited your Play Store reviews this month, the highest-leverage thing you can do today is the audit, not the next feature. Paste your app's URL into the [free Play Store analyzer](/tools/play-store-analyzer), run the eight-step checklist above, and write your three decisions. Most founders are surprised at what surfaces — both because the problems are smaller than they feared, and because the fixes are smaller than they had been postponing.

Five minutes. Once a week. Compounded over a year, it is the single highest-ROI operational habit in app development that does not require new tools, new hires, or new budget.
