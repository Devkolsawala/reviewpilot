You ran the sentiment analysis. You got back a chart that said "62 percent positive, 14 percent neutral, 24 percent negative." Now you are looking at it and wondering whether 62 percent is good or bad, whether 24 percent negative is alarming or normal, and what to actually do next.

This post is for the moment immediately after you have the analysis in front of you. Not a primer on what sentiment analysis is — you can find that on every blog. This is a practical interpretation guide, written by someone who has stared at hundreds of these breakdowns.

## What positive, neutral, and negative percentages actually mean

A sentiment classifier reads the language of a review and assigns it a label. It is not the same as the star rating. A 4-star review that says "I like the app overall but the OTP sometimes fails on Jio numbers" is usually classified as positive because the sentence majority is positive — but the operationally useful signal is the OTP complaint hiding inside.

Here is the calibration most Indian consumer apps should benchmark against in 2026:

| Positive % | Negative % | What it usually means |
|---|---|---|
| 80+ | Under 10 | Excellent. Verify sample size first — small samples skew positive. |
| 65 – 79 | 10 – 20 | Healthy. Focus shifts from triage to compounding gains. |
| 50 – 64 | 20 – 30 | Mixed. There is a specific issue. Find it before shipping anything else. |
| 35 – 49 | 30 – 45 | Structural problem. Either product, trust, or both are failing. |
| Under 35 | Over 45 | Crisis. Stop normal roadmap work, run the recovery playbook. |

Two things to watch for that the percentages themselves do not tell you:

**Sample skew.** If you only have 25 recent reviews and 15 of them are 5-star prompts your in-app review flow generated this week, your sentiment percentages look amazing and mean very little. Always cross-check against the total review count in the analyzer output.

**Time skew.** Sentiment usually analyzes the most recent 100 to 300 reviews. If your displayed rating has not moved in months but your sentiment looks excellent, your *recent* product is probably better than your historical average. That is good news — the rating will follow if you sustain the improvement.

## The response rate number — what's good, what's bad

Most analyzer outputs include a developer response rate. This is one of the highest-signal numbers in the entire breakdown, and it is the one most founders glance over.

**Below 30 percent reply rate.** You are operationally invisible to your users. Both Google's category algorithm and your prospective installers reading the review thread will downgrade you. Fix this before anything else, because the fix takes a week and the lift compounds for years.

**30 to 60 percent.** Inconsistent. Probably you reply when something is loud, then stop when life gets busy. The audit signal is "build a daily 15-minute reply slot." That alone moves the number to 80 percent within two weeks.

**60 to 85 percent.** Solid. Now focus on *quality* of replies — specific acknowledgment of the issue, not template apologies. The marginal lift from "did you reply" maxes out around 80 percent; above that, *what* you replied matters more than *whether* you replied.

**Above 85 percent.** Excellent. Operationally well-run. The remaining lift comes from speed, not coverage — getting first-reply time under 24 hours on every negative review.

The [reply rate ranking signal](/blog/review-reply-rate-play-store-ranking-signal) piece walks through why this number specifically maps to category ranking position. The short version: Google rewards apps that engage. The audit signal here matters operationally as much as algorithmically.

## The unreplied negative count — your priority queue

If only one number in the analyzer output makes it into next week's standup, it is this one. The count of unreplied 1- to 3-star reviews from the last 30 days. Why this number and not "total unreplied"?

- It is *recent enough* that the reviewer is likely still using the app.
- It is *negative enough* that an unanswered version of it is actively costing you installs from the next reader.
- It is *recoverable* — most reviewers in the last 30 days will update their review if you engage well.

Treat it as a priority queue, not a metric. The audit goal is to drive this number to zero each week. Specific reading:

- **0 to 5.** Acceptable. Clear by end of week.
- **6 to 20.** Build a daily slot. Will be cleared within a sprint.
- **21 to 50.** Blocking work for someone full-time for 2 to 4 days.
- **More than 50.** Active operational debt. Either you do not have enough reply capacity or your tooling is in the way. Both are solvable in a fortnight.

The recovery math is worth knowing: a contacted 1- or 3-star reviewer updates their review roughly 20 to 35 percent of the time when the reply specifically acknowledges their issue and indicates a fix. Each updated review removes a drag from your displayed average — more leverage than adding a new 5-star, because it changes both the numerator and the denominator's quality signal.

## Topic clustering — how to read which complaints matter most

Most sentiment analyzers will surface 5 to 8 topic clusters from your negative reviews. The clusters are the actionable layer. The percentages are the size of the problem; the clusters are the shape.

For each cluster, ask three questions:

**1. Is this a product, trust, support, or performance cluster?**

- **Product:** "feature X is broken," "missing Y," "Z does not work on Android 15"
- **Trust:** "feels like a scam," "money taken," "no one responds"
- **Support:** "9 days no reply," "chat bot useless," "escalation goes nowhere"
- **Performance:** "slow," "crashes," "battery drain," "app size"

The category determines who fixes it. Product clusters need engineering. Trust clusters need policy and communication. Support clusters need ops capacity. Performance clusters need a different engineering subteam than feature work.

**2. Is the cluster recent or persistent?**

A cluster that appears in reviews from the last 30 days only might be a regression from your latest release. A cluster that has been steady for 90+ days is structural — your competitors probably do not have it, which makes it both a problem and an opportunity once you fix it.

**3. How many reviews mention the cluster?**

A cluster of 4 reviews in a sample of 200 is noise. A cluster of 30 reviews in a sample of 200 is your roadmap. Most analyzers show cluster size; use it to prioritize.

A cluster that is product-tagged, persistent, and 20-percent-plus of the sample is the highest-priority item in your roadmap, regardless of what else the team had planned this sprint.

## When sentiment numbers lie

Three situations where the percentages will mislead you.

**Small sample on an in-app prompted review flow.** If most of your recent reviews come from your in-app prompt after a successful core flow, your sentiment will skew strongly positive and tell you nothing about your unhappy users — they uninstalled instead of reviewing. Cross-check with your support ticket volume and your refund rate.

**Review brigading or gaming.** Sudden spikes in either direction from a coordinated group. If you see 80 percent positive sentiment one week after a competitor relaunch, but no fundamental change in your product, look at the actual review bodies — coordinated positive reviews tend to use very similar language across many reviewers in a short window.

**Category-specific tone bias.** Fintech reviews are angrier than reading-app reviews. Food delivery reviews are angrier than language-learning reviews. Comparing your absolute positive percentage to a different category's number is misleading. Compare to category benchmarks, not global averages.

## Turning your sentiment breakdown into a one-week action plan

The 5-step template that works most often:

**Monday — diagnose.** Read the full analyzer output. Note positive percentage, response rate, unreplied negative count, top 3 complaint clusters. Write each on one line.

**Tuesday — reply backlog.** Clear all unreplied 1- to 3-star reviews from the last 30 days. Use specific, issue-acknowledging replies, not templates. This alone moves the response-rate number and starts the recovery loop on those reviewers.

**Wednesday — pick the cluster.** From your top 3 complaint clusters, pick exactly one to address this sprint. Resist the urge to fix all three. The fix can be a product change, a policy clarification, or a public communication — whatever the cluster category demands.

**Thursday — ship the cheapest version.** A v1 of the fix that addresses 60 percent of the cluster ships now. The remaining 40 percent goes into the next sprint.

**Friday — write the next week.** Re-run the sentiment analysis. Compare to Monday's snapshot. Note deltas. Pick next week's priority before logging off, so Monday morning is not a planning session.

Most teams that run this loop for 8 to 12 weeks see measurable shifts in both their sentiment numbers and their displayed rating. The reason it works is not the sophistication of the analysis — it is the discipline of acting on the numbers each week instead of every quarter.
