## The signals AI looks for — and why each one matters

Fake review detection AI is essentially a multi-signal classifier. It scores each reviewer and review on roughly 15–20 features, then surfaces high-risk reviews as candidates for human judgement. The major signals:

**1. Reviewer profile age.** Accounts created within the last 30–60 days that immediately review a business with no other activity are high-risk. Real customers usually have account histories — older profile, multiple unrelated reviews, photos uploaded somewhere.

**2. Review velocity spikes.** A business getting 2 reviews/week suddenly gets 8 reviews in 36 hours, all negative, from accounts with similar characteristics. The cluster itself is the signal — individual reviews might pass scrutiny.

**3. Linguistic similarity across accounts.** Three reviews from three different accounts using "unhygienic conditions," "horrible experience," and "would not recommend" in the same order is statistically improbable from independent customers. AI embedding-distance scores catch this in seconds.

**4. No photo or location history.** Genuine reviewers, especially in India in 2026, often have at least some photo contributions or location pins from past visits. Accounts with zero photo/location history and one review are statistically over-represented in fake review datasets.

**5. Geographic anomaly.** A restaurant in Pune getting a 1-star from an account whose other reviews are all in Kolkata or Chennai, with no travel pattern visible, is a soft anomaly. Combined with other signals it becomes strong.

**6. Reviewer pattern matching.** The same reviewer leaves 5-star reviews for the same 3 businesses (which may be the same chain, or paid reviewers' clients) and 1-star reviews for those businesses' competitors. This is the fingerprint of a paid reviewer.

**7. Tense and detail mismatch.** Real reviews tend to include specific, idiosyncratic details — staff name, dish, time of visit. Fake reviews are generic ("worst service ever, don't go") because the reviewer wasn't actually there.

**8. Timing patterns.** Reviews posted at 2 AM in clusters, or during the business's closed hours, or weeks after a "visit" date that doesn't match the business's actual operating history.

No single signal is conclusive. Eight weak signals stacked become statistically conclusive. This is exactly what AI does well that humans don't.

## Three types of fake reviews — different responses needed

Fake reviews are not one category. Treating them as one leads to the wrong response. There are three distinct types:

### Competitor attack campaigns

The most common in Indian competitive local niches. A competitor (or someone hired by them) commissions a wave of 1-star reviews timed to hit during your peak visibility — for restaurants, a Friday evening; for coaching centres, the day before admission deadlines. The reviews are often clustered in time, share linguistic patterns, and come from young accounts.

**Response:** AI surfaces the cluster, you flag each review through Google Business Profile with the "fake/spam" category, you file a Policy violation report citing the velocity spike and reviewer pattern. Google's trust team reviews and typically takes action within 7–14 days. We've seen 60–80% removal rates for well-documented competitor attack waves.

### Review farm purchases

Less common but more insidious. A business (sometimes the victim's own competitor, sometimes the victim itself in a different context) pays for reviews from a "review farm" — networks of accounts in Bangladesh, Pakistan, or Eastern Europe that produce reviews on demand. These are often 5-star fakes inflating a competitor's rating rather than hurting yours.

**Response:** Detecting these on a competitor's profile is harder because you don't see their reviewer data. Public-side signals: unusually high rating with no specific reviews, sudden velocity changes, geographic anomalies visible on reviewer profiles. Reporting to Google requires a third-party complaint, which has lower success rates (20–35%) but is worth doing when the evidence is strong.

### Incentivised reviews violating policy

A business runs a campaign: "Leave us a 5-star review and get a free coffee." This violates Google policy regardless of how it's framed. It's also extremely common in India in food and beauty verticals. Reviews resulting from incentives are technically fake even when the reviewer is real and had a real experience.

**Response:** If you spot a competitor doing this (e.g., a Reel showing them offering free items for reviews), report via Google's policy violation form with the evidence linked. Google's enforcement on this category has tightened significantly in 2026 — review removal rates run 70%+ when evidence is documented.

## How AI surfaces candidates, humans must act

A critical clarification: AI does not "remove" fake reviews. Only Google does. AI surfaces candidates with confidence scores. A human owner or reputation manager judges each one, makes the call to flag, and submits to Google.

The reason this two-step exists: false positives. AI might score a legitimate 1-star review as 0.7 fake-probability because the reviewer happens to have a young account and used generic phrasing. Removing legitimate negative feedback is its own policy violation and harms long-term trust. Human judgement on the surfaced candidates is the safety layer.

Practical workflow in a modern AI review management tool:

1. AI scores every new review on fake-probability (0.0 to 1.0)
2. Reviews above 0.6 get flagged in a "Suspicious Reviews" queue
3. Owner reviews the flag with the AI's reasoning ("Account age: 12 days, no other reviews, linguistic match to 2 other recent reviews")
4. Owner decides: flag to Google, ignore, or mark as legitimate
5. Action is logged for audit

This is the right division of labour. AI is doing what it's good at (pattern recognition at scale). Humans are doing what they're good at (judgement on edge cases). For more on AI-augmented review workflows generally, see our overview of [AI review replies](/blog/ai-vs-human-review-replies-2026).

## The escalation playbook — what to actually do

When you've identified a fake review (or wave), the escalation path:

**Step 1: Flag in Google Business Profile (5 minutes).**
Go to your GBP > Reviews > find the review > menu (three dots) > "Flag as inappropriate." Choose the most specific category: "Spam," "Conflict of interest," "Off-topic," or "Restricted content." Don't pick "I disagree with this review" — that's not a valid flag reason and gets ignored.

**Step 2: Wait 5–7 business days.**
Google's automated systems handle the first pass. About 30–40% of well-categorised flags get auto-removed in this window.

**Step 3: If still up, file a Policy violation report (15 minutes).**
Search "Google Business Profile policy violation report" and fill the form. Include:
- Business name and GBP URL
- Specific review URLs
- Evidence: screenshots of reviewer profiles showing young account ages, identical phrasing, etc.
- Pattern explanation: "All 7 reviews posted within 36 hours from accounts created within 60 days; 3 use near-identical phrasing"

This goes to a human reviewer at Google. Response time: 10–21 days. Success rate when evidence is well-documented: 60–80% for clear attack waves.

**Step 4: If still up after a month, escalate via Google support.**
GBP has phone support for verified business owners. Open a case, reference the previous report ticket numbers, ask for human escalation. This is the last lever short of legal action.

**Step 5: Counter-narrative in the meantime.**
While the removal is in process, the affected reviews are still public. Respond professionally to each one — short, non-defensive, offering a private channel. Future readers will see your replies as much as the reviews themselves.

## The Indian legal angle

Indian businesses do have legal recourse beyond Google's platform — though it's slow and rarely the first move. Two relevant laws:

**Information Technology Act, 2000 (and amendments).** Section 66 covers offences relating to false information posted online. Section 67 covers obscene/defamatory content. Filing a cyber-crime complaint with local police is possible when you have evidence of coordinated defamation (e.g., the competitor attack waves).

**Defamation under IPC (now BNS) Section 356.** Posting false statements that damage a business's reputation can be criminal defamation. Civil suits for damages are also possible.

In practice, legal action is the deterrent more than the remedy. Most fake review campaigns stop when the perpetrators realise the target is willing to escalate. We've seen Indian businesses send a formal cease-and-desist via a lawyer to a competitor — even before any court involvement — and have the fake review activity stop within 2 weeks.

## FAQ

**How accurate is AI at detecting fake Google reviews?**
For well-documented attack patterns (velocity spikes, account age clusters, linguistic similarity), modern AI detection runs 85–92% precision at the cluster level. Individual review classification is less accurate — typically 70–80% precision — which is why human judgement remains essential before flagging. The AI's job is to surface candidates, not to make the final call.

**Can Google detect fake reviews on its own?**
Yes, partially. Google's own spam detection removes millions of fake reviews automatically every year and has improved significantly since 2024. But the system is tuned for false-positive avoidance, which means many fakes pass through. Business-owner flags supplement Google's detection by providing specific evidence and pattern context the automated systems can't easily infer.

**What's the fastest way to get a fake review removed?**
Flag through GBP with the correct category, wait 5–7 days, then file a Policy violation report with documented pattern evidence (account ages, phrasing similarity, velocity timeline). Well-documented reports typically resolve in 10–21 days. Vague flags ("this review is wrong") generally fail; specific pattern evidence ("7 reviews in 36 hours from accounts <60 days old, 3 with identical phrasing") succeeds.

**Are AI-generated fake reviews harder to detect than human-written ones?**
Counterintuitively, often easier. AI-generated fake reviews tend to be generic, well-formed grammatically, and lack idiosyncratic detail — which is exactly the signature of a fake review. Detection AI catches these reliably. Human-written fakes from review farms are sometimes harder because they include specific (fabricated) details. The cluster signals (velocity, account age, reviewer pattern) catch both equally well.

**Should I report fake reviews on competitor profiles?**
You can, and the evidence threshold is the same. Google does accept third-party policy violation reports, though success rates are lower (20–35%) compared to owner-side reports. The strategic question is whether the time investment is worth it — for clearly egregious cases (a competitor with obvious fake review buying), yes. For minor discrepancies, your time is better spent on your own profile.
